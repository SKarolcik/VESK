Lights on.




Idea


From the moment we first open our eyes, light plays an essential role in our lives. Despite the fact that lighting conditions can affect your mood, productivity and even health, most of the light sources that we use are essentially decades-old technology. Inspired by modern displays which adjust brightness and hue based on ambient conditions, our SmartBulb takes this idea to your living room, providing you with lighting that suits all your needs and occasions, ensuring consistency through feedback from a highly accurate RGB sensor.

But what does it mean changing colour temperature?

Colour temperature is a way to describe the light appearance provided by a light bulb (lamp). It is measured in degrees of Kelvin (K) on a scale from 1,000 to 10,000. A lamp’s colour temperature lets us know what the look and feel of the light produced will be.

And what colour temperature is right for you?

At the lower end of the scale, from 2000K to 3000K, the light produced is called “warm white” and ranges from orange to yellow-white in appearance. It makes us feel very comfortable in our home, therefore it is suitable in bedrooms, living rooms, restaurants etc.

Colour temperatures between 3100K and 4500K are referred to as “cool white” or “bright white.” Light bulbs (lamps) within this range will emit a more neutral white light and may even have a slightly blue tint. Studies show that working under these lighting conditions makes us very productive especially for work during the night, it feels as if we were studying during the day. It is recommended to use for studying, in the offices, bathrooms etc.

Above 4500K brings us into the “daylight” colour temperature of light. Light bulbs (lamps) with colour temperatures of 4500K and above will give off a blue-white light that mimics daylight. This colour temperature will make you even more focused and it is suitable for reading and task lighting.

Our product







Sense


Currently using a lightweight RGB sensor TCS3472 by adafruit, that is packed with amazing features:

  • Red, Green, Blue (RGB), and clear light sensing with IR blocking filter
  • Amazing sensitivity with 3,800,000:1 dynamic range
  • Very low power, only 2.5 µA sleep state and 65µA wait state with programmable wait state time from 2.4 ms to > 7 seconds

Communication with our microchip is done through I2C channel. Drivers for accesing the sensor are written completely by us (no library functions, we wanted to be as efficient as possible!).

Process

Data from the sensor arrives to ESP8266 Wi-Fi chip. It extracts the meanigful information (like current colour value changes) and create a JSON formatted message that is subsequently sent to the cloud through MQTT protocol.

Processing was designed in a way to minimize power consumption, and therefore when the conditions in your room become stable, the device enters a polling loop that only check for changes every now and then and does not maintain a continuos connection over MQTT.

Our prototype already supports changing modes through your smartphone when you publish to a desired MQTT channel. Currently there are 4 different modes:

  • Warm: Slightly more yellow/red-ish tint, usually used for relax
  • Neutral: Maintains a bright white light conditions
  • Cold: Blue-ish mode, good for productivity and also helps to keep you awake
  • DISCO!: Turn your bedroom into a fancy club with just one simple click

For future improvements we plan on adding brightness control as well as a standalone smartphone app that would be handling the publishing to the correct MQTT channels for you




Adjust


In order to compensate for the ambient color conditions, we use a ring of powerful RGB LEDs that can illuminate an entire room with any desired color to keep the light consistent. For prototyping purposes, we use an LED ring called Neopixels that contains 24 individually accessible LEDs. We use information available from the datasheet as well as our own measurements to estimate the power consumption for any instantaneous mode of operation, and send this information to the cloud which keeps track of total energy savings compared to baseline operation. The user will be able to monitor this with our upcoming app (soonTM).
